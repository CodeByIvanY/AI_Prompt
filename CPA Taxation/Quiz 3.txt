Question 3 
Michael King is an employee of Conron Inc., a Canadian-controlled private corporation (CCPC). He was granted 5,000 options on January 1, Year 2022. Each option entitled Michael to purchase one share of Conron for $5.00. On the grant date, Conron shares were valued at $6.00 per share. Michael exercised his option to acquire 5,000 shares on March 1, Year 2023, when the shares were valued at $8.00 per share. On October 1, 2024, Michael sold 5,000 shares at $9.70 per share.
How much will Michael’s taxable income increase by for 2024 as a result of the above transactions?
a) $4,250
b) $11,750
c) $14,250
d) $19,25250
Feedback
Option d) is correct. The stock option benefit is calculated as ($8.00 – $5.00) × 5,000 = $15,000, and the taxable capital gain is calculated as [($9.70 – $8.00) × 5,000] × ½ = $4,250. The option was in the money on the grant date because the FMV on the grant date of $6.00 was greater than the exercise price of $5.00. Therefore, as an employee of a CCPC, Michael will be eligible for a Division C deduction only if he holds the shares for at least 24 months. Michael held the shares from March 1 of 2023 to October 1 of 2024, which is less than 24 months. Therefore, Michael is not eligible for a Division C deduction.

Question 10 
Sharon is a member of her employer’s money purchase Registered Pension Plan (RPP). Sharon’s 2023 earned income was $70,000, and her employer indicated that her 2023 pension adjustment (PA) is $3,000. At the end of 2023, Sharon had no Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) deduction room carryforward, nor any undeducted contributions available to carry forward. The RRSP dollar limit for 2024 is $31,560. On May 15, 2024, Sharon contributed $6,000 to her RRSP. Sharon contributed a further $6,100 on January 3, 2025.
Which one of the following is the maximum RRSP deduction that Sharon can claim on her 2024 personal tax return?
a) $6,000
b) $9,600
c) $11,600
d) $12,600

Feedback
Option b) is correct. $12,600 – $3,000 = $9,600. $12,600 is 18% of Sharon's earned income for 2023; $3,000 is her PA. Sharon's maximum deduction is the lesser of her deduction limit of $9,600 and contributions made in the available time period of $6,000 + $6,100 = $12,100.

Question 13 
Early 2024, Ed found a new job that required him to relocate from Saskatoon to Edmonton. He incurred the following costs with respect to the move:
Cost of flight and cab fare for house-hunting trip to Edmonton (Note 1) $ 545
Lease cancellation costs on Saskatoon home 590
Cost of moving household effects 2,180
Legal fees and title transfer fees on Edmonton home 1,860
Note 1: Ed made a one-day trip to Edmonton to find a new home. He had done significant research prior to the trip, so with the help of a real estate agent, it took only one day to finalize the purchase of a home.
Assuming that Ed earned sufficient employment income after the move, which one of the following is the maximum claim Ed may make for moving expenses?

a) $2,770
b) $3,315
c) $4,630
d) $5,175

Feedback
Option a) is correct. The maximum deduction is $2,770, which includes the lease cancellation costs on the Saskatoon home of $590 and the cost of moving household effects of $2,180. The cost of the house-hunting trip is not deductible, and the legal fees and title transfer fees on the Edmonton home are not deductible because the Saskatoon home was not owned (it was rented).
Question 14

Feedback
